Chapter 3

Software Safety: Liability and Practice

Why Software Safety Matters

  • Software now controls transport, healthcare, industry, defense, and energy systems.
  • Failure in these environments can put human life at risk.
  • That is why safety-critical software must be engineered more carefully than ordinary business software.

What Software Safety Means

  • Software safety is about preventing harm to people and the environment.
  • It goes beyond correctness into resilience and fault tolerance.
  • The goal is to fail safely, not dangerously.

Safety-Critical Systems and Liability

  • Safety-critical systems are systems whose failure can cause injury or death.
  • Examples include air traffic control, medical systems, and nuclear systems.
  • When software fails, a key legal question is: who is responsible?

Liability Concepts

Fault-based ideas

  • Negligence
  • Product liability
  • Breach of contract or warranty

Broader responsibility

  • Strict liability
  • Vicarious liability
  • Personal liability

These concepts decide whether responsibility depends on careless behaviour, defective products, employment relationships, or broken promises.

Risk Mitigation and Professional Duty

  • Use hazard analysis, formal verification, testing, redundancy, and clear safety documentation.
  • Engineers must protect the public beyond the legal minimum.
  • Case studies like Therac-25 and the Boeing 737 MAX show the cost of weak safeguards, weak testing, and poor accountability.
Takeaway

In safety-critical software, ethical responsibility and legal liability are tightly connected.
