Chapter 7

Social and Ethical Implications of Software Engineering

Ethics in Software Engineering

Ethics studies ideas like right, wrong, good, and harm. In software engineering, it becomes a set of principles that guide how we design and build systems for people.

  • It protects welfare, justice, safety, and trust.
  • It affects choices made during requirements, design, coding, testing, and release.
  • It reminds engineers that software decisions are never purely technical.

Why Ethics Matters

Ethical failures in software can create real-world harm at enormous scale.

  • The Cambridge Analytica scandal exposed personal data and trust failures.
  • Software failures in safety-critical systems, such as aviation, can cost lives.
  • Bad design or careless data use can violate privacy, fairness, and public safety.
Remember

As software spreads into daily life, ethical quality matters as much as technical quality.

Why Ethical Judgment Is Hard

  • Ethics is context-dependent; one answer does not fit every case.
  • Many dilemmas are not obviously right or wrong at first glance.
  • Rules and standards help, but they do not replace thoughtful judgment.
  • Engineers must keep analysing the broader social situation around the software.

Engineers' Ethical Responsibilities

  • Act in the public interest, not only in the employer's short-term interest.
  • Approve software only when it is sufficiently tested, safe, and reliable.
  • Evaluate likely impacts on users, bystanders, and society before and after release.
  • Protect privacy and avoid preventable harm to people or the environment.

Fairness and Non-Discrimination

  • Engineers should not create systems that exclude or disadvantage groups unfairly.
  • Accessible and internationalised design helps prevent hidden exclusion.
  • Fairness also means giving users ways to appeal, report, and correct harmful outcomes.
Practical examples

Language support, screen-reader compatibility, and bias complaint channels are ethical design features, not optional extras.

Privacy and Data Ethics

  • Collect only the data that is actually needed: data minimisation.
  • Be transparent about what is collected, why it is collected, and how long it stays.
  • Respect consent, access, correction, and deletion rights.
  • Security is part of privacy: poor protection is an ethical failure too.

ACM Code: General Principles

The ACM Code of Ethics gives a broad foundation for computing professionals.

Core values

  • Contribute to society and human well-being
  • Avoid harm
  • Be honest and trustworthy
  • Be fair and do not discriminate

Respect and rights

  • Honor intellectual property
  • Give proper credit
  • Respect privacy
  • Honor confidentiality

ACM Professional Responsibilities and Leadership

  • Maintain competence and respect relevant laws.
  • Accept review, analyse risks, and design systems that are robustly secure.
  • Leaders must keep the public good central in team decisions.
  • Users and affected people should have their needs clearly considered in requirements and design.

IEEE/ACM Software Engineering Code

This code gives software-specific guidance through eight principles:

  • Public, Client and Employer, Product, and Judgment.
  • Management, Profession, Colleagues, and Self.
  • Together they push engineers toward high standards, integrity, and lifelong learning.

Resolving Ethical Conflicts

  • Utilitarianism asks which choice leads to the best overall consequences.
  • Deontology asks which duties or moral rules must be respected.
  • When principles clash, use reasoned judgment, discuss with peers, and return to the code.
Exam angle

Codes do not give a formula for every dilemma. They give values and structure for making defensible choices.

Social Responsibility of Engineers

Software shapes news feeds, hiring, lending, transport, and healthcare, so engineers affect society whether they intend to or not.

  • Ask: Who benefits? Who is harmed? Who is left out?
  • Do not treat technology as value-neutral.
  • Consider even the least empowered stakeholders.

Algorithmic Bias and Its Sources

  • Data bias comes from skewed or historically unfair training data.
  • Algorithmic bias comes from feature choices, objectives, or optimisation goals.
  • User bias enters through human interaction patterns such as ratings or feedback loops.
Classic example

Amazon's hiring model learned from historically male-dominated resumes and ended up disadvantaging women.

Fairness: Group vs. Individual

Group fairness

  • Looks for parity across groups
  • Useful against disparate impact
  • Example: similar approval rates by gender

Individual fairness

  • Treat similar people similarly
  • Closer to personal justice
  • Harder to define and enforce

These two ideas can conflict, so engineers must choose and document the fairness target that fits the context.

Bias Trade-Offs, Privacy, and Law

  • Improving fairness can sometimes reduce raw predictive accuracy.
  • Not all bias is automatically unethical, but bias against protected groups is a major concern.
  • Laws such as GDPR reinforce rights around consent, access, deletion, and explanation.
  • Ethics often goes beyond the law: legal compliance is the floor, not the ceiling.

Bias Auditing and Real-World Examples

  • Toolkits such as IBM AIF360 and Microsoft Fairlearn help measure and mitigate bias.
  • Facial-recognition systems have shown higher error rates on darker-skinned and female faces.
  • Generative AI can reproduce stereotypes, such as skewed images for jobs like "CEO".
Remember

Ethical engineers audit models before release and continue monitoring them after data or models change.

Accountability and Inclusive Design

  • Document data sources, design assumptions, and known limitations.
  • If a system causes harm, detect it, report it, and fix it.
  • Test with diverse users, including people with disabilities and users in low-bandwidth settings.
  • Accessibility features such as captions and alt-text are part of ethical quality.

Fairness Metrics and Best Practices

  • Common metrics include demographic parity and equal opportunity.
  • Compare error rates or positive rates across groups to spot unfair patterns.
  • Integrate fairness checks into the lifecycle just like security and testing checks.

Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions

Hofstede's framework explains how national values influence behaviour, communication, trust, and system expectations.

  • The six dimensions are PDI, IDV, MAS, UAI, LTO, and IVR.
  • They affect UX design, software adoption, error handling, and collaboration features.
  • Ignoring culture can lower adoption and create accidental bias.

PDI and IDV in Software

Power Distance (PDI)

  • High PDI: users expect hierarchy, authority, clear official guidance
  • Low PDI: users expect transparency, questions, and customisation

Individualism (IDV)

  • Individualist contexts: personal dashboards, private settings
  • Collectivist contexts: group workflows, shared goals, social proof

MAS and UAI in Software

Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS)

  • Masculine cultures often respond to rankings, status, and competition
  • Feminine cultures tend to prefer cooperation, support, and well-being

Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI)

  • High UAI: detailed instructions, predictable behaviour, strong defaults
  • Low UAI: experimentation, beta features, flexible exploration

LTO, IVR, and Practical Cultural Design

  • LTO: long-term cultures value planning and future rewards; short-term cultures want quick wins and immediate feedback.
  • IVR: indulgent cultures welcome playful and expressive UX; restrained cultures may prefer moderation and sober flows.
  • Practical checklist: identify target culture, map traits to UI choices, localise beyond translation, offer customisation, and test with real users.
Australia vs. Saudi Arabia

Australia trends lower in power distance and higher in individualism, while Saudi Arabia trends higher in power distance and uncertainty avoidance. That means the same learning app may need more authority cues and structured guidance in one market, and more autonomy in the other.

Chapter 8

Emerging Software Development Paradigms

The Modern Architecture Imperative

  • As systems grow, business complexity breaks simple vertically layered designs.
  • One giant model becomes a monolithic mess where small changes have huge side effects.
  • Modern systems need to be decomposable, elastic, reactive, and business-centric.

Defining Domain-Driven Design (DDD)

DDD is Eric Evans's approach for handling complex business domains by making software models reflect the business reality directly.

  • Focus on the core domain and domain logic.
  • Work closely with domain experts to refine the model.
  • Treat DDD as both a design discipline and a team culture.

Strategic Pillars and Ubiquitous Language

  • Identify the most valuable part of the business: the core domain.
  • Create a ubiquitous language shared by developers and domain experts.
  • Accept that no single model can represent the entire enterprise, so boundaries are necessary.
Remember

If the business calls it Transfer Authorization, the code should also call it TransferAuthorization.

Bounded Contexts

  • A Bounded Context is an explicit boundary where one model and one language stay consistent.
  • The same term can mean different things in different contexts.
  • Each bounded context is a strong candidate for a separate team or microservice.

Context Map

  • A Context Map shows how bounded contexts relate and translate to each other.
  • Important patterns include Customer/Supplier, Shared Kernel, and Conformist.
  • It helps architects decide when communication should be synchronous APIs versus asynchronous events.

Tactical DDD Patterns

Once boundaries are clear, tactical DDD gives patterns for building clean domain code inside them.

  • Entities
  • Value Objects
  • Aggregates and Aggregate Roots
  • Domain Services and Domain Events

Entities vs. Value Objects

Entities

  • Have identity over time
  • Usually mutable
  • Example: Employee #4501

Value Objects

  • Defined only by their values
  • Immutable
  • Examples: Address, Money, DateRange

Aggregates and the Aggregate Root

  • An Aggregate groups related entities and value objects into one consistency boundary.
  • The Aggregate Root is the only object outsiders talk to directly.
  • The root enforces invariants and repositories save or load the aggregate through it.
Key term

Aggregate changes should commit together as one transaction so business rules remain valid.

Domain Services and Domain Events

  • Domain Services hold logic that spans multiple aggregates.
  • Domain Events record important facts that already happened, so they are named in the past tense.
  • Events are often published after a successful state change and become a bridge to event-driven systems.

DDD's Architectural Impact

  • DDD helps break monoliths into clean microservice boundaries.
  • Services become cohesive inside and loosely coupled outside.
  • Tactical patterns keep service code more testable, expressive, and aligned with the business.

Event-Driven Architecture (EDA) Basics

  • EDA is a style where the core activity is producing, detecting, consuming, and reacting to events.
  • Systems become reactive: they respond to things that happened instead of waiting on direct requests.
  • EDA is valuable for high throughput, low latency, and loose coupling.

Events, Commands, and Immutability

  • An event is a fact about the past; a command is a request for the future.
  • Events usually carry a small payload such as type, time, and affected identity.
  • Events are treated as immutable historical records.

Asynchronous Communication and Event Brokers

  • Producers publish without waiting for consumers, which improves responsiveness.
  • An event broker such as Kafka, RabbitMQ, or Amazon SQS routes the events.
  • This decouples producers and consumers in both time and space.

Publish-Subscribe and Event Streaming

  • In Pub/Sub, producers publish to topics and many consumers can subscribe.
  • Persistent logs let consumers replay old events for resilience.
  • Streaming platforms process massive live flows such as clickstreams, IoT data, or logs.

EDA Benefits and the Integration Layer

  • Services can scale independently based on their own workloads.
  • The broker acts as a buffer, improving resilience when consumers fail temporarily.
  • EDA future-proofs the system because new consumers can be added without changing producers.

Serverless and Function-as-a-Service (FaaS)

  • Serverless does not remove servers; it hides server management behind the cloud provider.
  • FaaS runs small stateless functions in response to triggers.
  • Developers focus on business logic instead of OS patching and server administration.

Consumption Model and Elasticity

  • FaaS charges per request and compute time, so there is no idle cost.
  • Platforms can burst-scale to many parallel instances, then scale back to zero.
  • This fits sporadic or unpredictable workloads especially well.

State, Triggers, and Security

  • Serverless functions are designed to be stateless; persistent state lives in databases, queues, or caches.
  • Common triggers include HTTP requests, database changes, file uploads, and queue messages.
  • Security follows a shared responsibility model: the provider secures infrastructure, while the team secures code, IAM, encryption, and configuration.

The Synergistic Architecture

  • DDD defines what we build: the business model and service boundaries.
  • EDA defines how services integrate: asynchronously and loosely coupled.
  • Serverless/FaaS defines where and how the logic runs: elastically and pay-per-use.
Big picture

Together, these three paradigms aim for systems that are business-aligned, resilient, scalable, and cost-efficient.

FaaS as an Event Consumer

A common modern loop is simple:

  • An Aggregate Root publishes a Domain Event.
  • The broker routes it to interested services.
  • A dedicated function in another bounded context wakes up, performs the needed logic, and stops.
Three-pillar recap

DDD models the business, EDA moves the signals, and Serverless executes the reaction.
