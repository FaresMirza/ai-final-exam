Chapter 1

Emerging Trends in Software Engineering

The Dynamic Landscape

Software engineering is no longer just writing code — it's about orchestrating intelligent systems, cloud-native architectures, and human-centric solutions driving global digital transformation.

  • Market context: global software spend projected to surpass $1.3 trillion (Gartner, 2025).
  • Innovation drivers: AI, quantum breakthroughs, and DevOps automation are pushing release cycles from months to days.
  • Responsibility: Ethical AI, sustainability, and inclusive design are now non-negotiable.

AI-Driven Development & Automation

AI is now embedded across the entire software lifecycle, shifting developer focus from repetitive coding to strategic problem-solving.

  • Ubiquitous AI tools: 80%+ of developers use AI tools daily; 90% report productivity gains.
  • Evolving roles, not replacements: AI handles boilerplate & debugging; engineers focus on architecture, verification, and AI oversight.
Remember

The keyword is augmentation, not replacement. Junior developers remain central to long-term growth.

Vibe Coding

Coined by Andrej Karpathy in Feb 2025. Conversational, natural-language-driven code generation — developers steer LLMs with prompts and focus on high-level feedback instead of writing code line by line.

Easy example

Telling an LLM "build me a to-do app with dark mode" and iterating by feel, instead of typing every component yourself.

Remember — the risks

Fast prototyping comes with unpredictable outputs, maintainability concerns, and security flaws.

Agentic AI for Collaborative Engineering

Autonomous AI agents that act as team members — assisting with requirements gathering, design, code generation, and testing. They handle complex decisions but need AI-based V&V (Verification & Validation) to keep them honest.

Intelligent Testing & Debugging

  • SonarQube + ML regression analysis → predictive bug detection.
  • Auto-fixing syntax errors and generating test cases.
  • Result: faster, more reliable releases.
Case example

GitHub reports 46% of code written on its platform in 2025 involved Copilot assistance.

DevOps & GitOps Evolution

  • DevOps → Platform Engineering: internal developer platforms (IDPs), self-service tools, golden-path workflows reduce cognitive load.
  • DevSecOps: security integrated at every lifecycle stage.
  • Microservices & containers: Docker, Kubernetes — portability and scalability.
  • Edge, IoT, real-time: on-device AI inference cuts latency; less reliance on central clouds.
Remember

Businesses adopting GitOps cut deployment lead time by 40–60% (CNCF, 2024). Master CI/CD with Jenkins, ArgoCD, Flux.

Microservices, Serverless & Event-Driven Architectures

Cloud-native computing dominates 2025 — combining modularity, scalability, and cost-efficiency.

  • Microservices: independent scaling, language flexibility.
  • Serverless: AWS Lambda, Google Cloud Functions — event-driven, pay-as-you-go.
  • EDA: 72% of enterprises use it, only 13% are mature (Gartner, 2025).
  • Service mesh: Istio, Linkerd, Envoy — zero-trust security, fine-grained access control, cert rotation.
  • WASM: Fermyon pushes WebAssembly for portable, lightweight serverless workloads.
Industry note

Kubernetes remains the backbone of orchestration, now supporting hybrid + AI-driven workflows.

Quantum Computing Integration

Milestone in 2025: Microsoft's Majorana 1 Quantum Chip (Feb 2025) demonstrates topological qubits for industrial-scale computing.

  • Why quantum? Classical computing is hitting transistor-miniaturisation limits; quantum solves previously intractable problems (molecule simulation, logistics).
  • Applications: drug discovery (protein folding), finance (portfolio optimisation), cybersecurity (both a threat — breaking RSA — and a solution — quantum-safe encryption).
Career prep

Experienced devs → learn Q# (Microsoft) and Qiskit (IBM). Newer devs → focus on fundamentals; mainstream adoption is still 3–5 years away.

Sustainable Software Engineering

Sustainability is a core engineering requirement by 2025. Google DeepMind's AI optimisations cut data-centre cooling energy use by up to 40% in 2024.

  • Energy-efficient algorithms — optimise for lower CPU cycles and power.
  • Green cloud — AWS, Azure, GCP committing to carbon neutrality.
  • Lifecycle assessment — measure carbon footprint across dev, deploy, retire.
Impact

Software accounts for ~2–3% of global CO₂ emissions; efficient code & sustainable cloud reduce both cost and environmental harm.

Enhanced Security & Privacy Practices

With attacks rising 50% YoY (CheckPoint, 2025), security-first engineering dominates.

DevSecOps

  • Vuln scans in every commit
  • Shift-left security

Zero Trust

  • "Never trust, always verify"
  • Continuous authentication
Remember

Privacy engineering = built-in compliance (GDPR, CCPA, AI ethics) — not an afterthought.

Human-Centric Software Engineering

Beyond technology, human needs drive design.

  • Inclusive UX — accessibility baked in from day one.
  • Adaptive interfaces — context-aware personalisation (IoT + AI).
  • Ethical AI — transparent decisions, bias mitigation, fairness.

Low-Code & No-Code Platforms

Democratise software creation — business users build apps with minimal coding. They accelerate digital transformation, reduce backlogs, and foster cross-functional collaboration.

  • Visual development — drag-and-drop editors for rapid prototyping.
  • API integration — seamless connectivity to data & third-party services.
  • Governance & security — enhanced controls for compliance & data protection.

Future Skills & Professional Roles

  • Evolving career pathways — demand splits into generalists (cross-org challenges) and specialists (deep domain).
  • Strategic thinking — engineers define what to build and why; AI handles implementation details.
  • Continual upskilling — AI toolchains, platform engineering, sustainable programming, security, quantum & agentic AI.

How to Keep Yourself Updated

  • Experiment with new technology.
  • Attend national and international conferences.
  • Read technology-related books.
  • Keep practising your existing tech.
  • Join developer communities.
  • Collaborate with other developers.

Will Software Engineers Stay in Demand?

  • 1. Rapid tech advancements — AI, quantum, 5G, IoT, AR/VR all rely on software.
  • 2. The world is moving online — post-pandemic transformation; apps like Zoom, Uber, Paytm need skilled engineers.
  • 3. Code requires continuous updates — software is never "done"; constant upgrades and rewrites.
  • 4. Role across the product lifecycle — design, development, testing, deployment, docs, maintenance.
  • 5. Shortage of skilled engineers — demand exceeds supply → higher salaries.
Remember

Industry needs more than coding skills: SDLC knowledge, problem-solving, teamwork, and trend awareness.

Chapter 2

Software Products

What Is a Software Product?

Software products are generic software systems that deliver functionality useful to a range of customers — from large business systems (MS Excel) to personal tools (Evernote) to mobile apps and games (Sudoku).

Remember

Product methods evolved from techniques originally built for one-off, custom software. Custom systems are still vital for large businesses and government bodies.

Project-Based Software Engineering

Software is built for an external client who owns the requirements.

  • Starting point: a set of requirements from the customer.
  • Developer: a software company (contractor) designs and implements to meet them.
  • Change is normal: the customer can change requirements anytime; the contractor adapts.
  • Long lifetime: custom software often lives 10+ years and must be supported.
The triangle (Fig 1.1)

Problem (customer) → generates → Requirements (customer & developer) → implemented-by → Software (developer) → helps-with → back to Problem.

Product Software Engineering

No external client — the developer spots a business opportunity, builds a product, and sells it to many customers.

  • Developer-driven: the same company decides timeline, features, and when to change them.
  • Speed matters: rapid delivery is essential to capture the market.
The triangle (Fig 1.2)

Opportunity (developer) → inspires → Product features (developer) → implemented-by → Software (developer) → realizes → back to Opportunity.

Product Lines vs Platforms

Software Product Line

  • A family sharing a common core
  • Each member adds customer-specific bits
  • Used when a generic product can't meet specific needs

Platform

  • Software (or sw+hw) that other apps build on
  • Provides extensible functionality
  • Example: Facebook + Facebook apps

Software Execution Models

Where does the product actually run? Three options:

  • Stand-alone — runs entirely on the customer's computer. Vendor only pushes updates.
  • Hybrid — part on the customer's machine, part on the vendor's servers (e.g. data backups, extra features).
  • Software as a Service (SaaS) — all functionality on the vendor's servers; customer uses a browser or app.
Remember

SaaS = everything remote. Stand-alone = everything local. Hybrid = both — common for cloud-synced consumer apps.

Comparable Software Development

Other dev types share the "no external customer paying" trait with product development:

  • Student projects — students decide the features for an assignment.
  • Research software — researchers build tools to answer their own questions.
  • Internal tools — devs build for their own team; not for external release.

The Product Vision

The starting point for product development. A short statement that captures the product's essence and answers three questions:

  • What is the product to be developed?
  • Who are the target customers and users?
  • Why should customers buy it?

Moore's Vision Template

A fill-in-the-blanks structure for writing a sharp product vision:

  • FOR (target customer)
  • WHO (need / opportunity)
  • The (PRODUCT NAME) is a (product category)
  • THAT (key benefit / compelling reason to buy)
  • UNLIKE (primary competitive alternative)
  • OUR PRODUCT (primary differentiation)

Vision Example — iLearn

Filled-in vision

FOR teachers and educators WHO need a way to help students use web-based learning resources, THE iLearn system is an open learning environment THAT lets teachers configure resources per class/student. UNLIKE Moodle and other VLEs that focus on admin, OUR PRODUCT focuses on the learning process itself — enabling subject-/age-specific environments built from any web resources.

Why this format works

It forces clarity on customer, problem, category, benefit, competitor, and differentiator — all in one paragraph.

Information Sources for a Vision

  • Domain experience — developers who already work in the area spot gaps and frustrations.
  • Product experience — users of existing software see simpler/better ways (often enabled by new tech, e.g. speech).
  • Customer experience — discussions with prospective customers reveal their problems and constraints.
  • Prototyping & playing around — build a quick prototype to refine your idea before committing.

Software Product Management

A business activity centred on the products a company sells.

  • Product managers (PMs) own the product — planning, development, marketing.
  • They sit at the interface between the company, its customers, and the dev team.
  • Involved at every stage — from initial concept to withdrawing the product from the market.
Remember

PMs must look outward to customers, not inward at the code.

Product Management Concerns

PMs balance three pulls (Fig 1.4):

Business Needs

  • Software must hit the company's business goals

Technology Constraints

  • Make devs aware of tech issues that matter to customers
Customer Experience

Stay in regular contact with current & potential customers — understand what they're looking for, who they are, and how they use the product.

Technical Interactions of PMs

Day-to-day technical responsibilities of a product manager:

  • Product vision management — guard against "vision drift" when evaluating changes.
  • Product roadmap — own the plan for development, release, and marketing.
  • User stories & scenarios — refine the vision into concrete features.
  • Product backlog — prioritised "to-do" list; PM helps set priorities.
  • Acceptance testing — verify a release meets roadmap goals and is efficient/reliable.
  • Customer testing — pick customers, run early-release sessions, gather feedback.
  • UI design — act as a surrogate user; check that features aren't unnecessarily complex.

Product Prototyping

Build an early version to test ideas and convince funders the product has real potential. Users can't react to a vision statement, but they can react to a working demo.

  • A prototype helps identify fundamental components and test technology.
  • Building one should be the first thing you do.
  • Plan to throw it away — re-implement properly for security and reliability.

Feasibility Demo

  • Prove the idea works
  • Convince funders/management
  • Show what beats competitors

Customer Demo

  • Extend feasibility prototype
  • Add customer-specific features
  • Requires user studies first
Remember

Prototyping is for learning — refine your ideas, demo features to potential customers, then rebuild the real thing.
