Chapter 5

Sustainability in Software Engineering

Defining Sustainability

General (Brundtland, 1987): "Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs."

  • In Software Engineering — "the capacity to endure" (Becker et al., 2016).
  • A quality concern alongside usability, safety, and security.
  • Covers long-term environmental, social, economic, individual, and technical effects.
  • Must be a first-class concern across requirements, design, implementation, testing, and deployment.

Why Sustainability Matters

  • Digital tech accounts for 2–4% of global energy use and greenhouse gas emissions.
  • Sustainable software lessens hardware energy demand and boosts overall efficiency.
  • Regulations and standards increasingly mandate measurable sustainability practices.
  • Long-lasting, maintainable software reduces waste — and societal expectations now require it.
Remember

Sustainability fosters innovation, competitiveness, and resilience — it's not just an environmental ask.

Karlskrona Manifesto — Vision, Mission, Values

An international group of researchers' commitment to embedding sustainability in IT design.

  • Vision — revolutionise how IT products/services are designed to sustain and regenerate human and natural systems.
  • Mission — develop research and training that empowers IT pros to embed sustainability in design.
  • Values — awareness through collaboration, diverse perspectives, positive impact on the world.

The Nine Karlskrona Principles

  • P1 Systemic — never an isolated property; systemic thinking is the starting point.
  • P2 Multidimensional — economic, social, environmental, technical, individual.
  • P3 Interdisciplinary — collaboration across disciplines.
  • P4 Transcends the system's objective — even when sustainability isn't the system's primary goal.
  • P5 Applies to system + broader context — design covers both the system itself and its impact on environment.
  • P6 Action at several levels — different interventions, different impact.
  • P7 Multiple time scales — assess across timeframes; long-term indicators matter.
  • P8 Long-term ≠ sacrifice — innovation can serve both present and future.
  • P9 Visibility — system's social position and context must be visible.

The Five Sustainability Dimensions

Environmental

  • Protect natural resources from human needs/wastes

Economic

  • Positive economic value and capital growth

Social

  • Equitable, diverse, connected, democratic relationships

Technical

  • Tech that handles change/evolution efficiently
Individual (the 5th)

Individual needs protected and supported with dignity — developments should improve human life, not threaten it.

Environmental Dimension

  • Minimise energy, emissions, waste across the lifecycle.
  • Optimise software to lower data-centre energy & hardware load.
  • Use low-resource algorithms and efficient runtimes.
  • Measure software-related carbon footprints; extend hardware lifespan; tackle e-waste via circular economy.

Economic Dimension

  • Deliver value without unsustainable financial burdens.
  • Maintainable codebases prevent costly technical debt.
  • Reuse open-source components to cut dev cost.
  • Evaluate full lifecycle costs — maintenance, support, disposal — not just development.

Social Dimension

  • Equitable access for diverse global user communities.
  • Inclusive design — cultural, linguistic, accessibility considerations.
  • Trust via privacy/security; fair labour in supply chains.
  • Prevent reinforcing inequalities through responsible deployment.

Individual Dimension

  • Addresses physical & mental wellbeing of devs, maintainers, users.
  • Humane workloads, ergonomic interfaces, low cognitive load.
  • Respects privacy, autonomy, sense of agency.
  • Developer satisfaction → sustained high-quality outcomes.

Technical Dimension

  • Architectures for longevity, maintainability, scalability.
  • Modularity + interoperability; standardised protocols.
  • Minimise dependencies on short-lived tech (avoid forced migrations).
  • Security & privacy by design; CI/CD to keep systems dependable.

Three Types of Sustainability Impacts

  • Direct — immediate effects (e.g. compressing images cuts storage and transfer energy).
  • Indirect — long-term enabling effects (e.g. teleconferencing reduces travel emissions).
  • Rebound — efficiency gains drive more usage (e.g. cheaper cloud storage leads to data hoarding).
Remember

The rebound effect is the sneaky one — efficiency without behavioural restraint can increase total resource demand.

Case: Volkswagen Emissions Scandal

What happened

VW installed "defeat device" software in millions of diesel cars. During lab tests, cars looked compliant; on the road they emitted up to 40× more NOx. The software falsified sustainability metrics, misleading regulators and consumers.

Lesson

Software ethics ⇄ environmental sustainability. Billions in fines + reputational damage = the hidden cost of dishonest engineering.

Requirements Engineering for Sustainability

  • Define functional + non-functional requirements — including explicit sustainability goals.
  • Capture energy efficiency and lifecycle resource metrics during elicitation.
  • Use goal-oriented modelling to link sustainability concerns to software objectives.
  • Involve diverse stakeholders to understand social/environmental impacts early.
  • Document trade-offs between competing requirements transparently.

Sustainable Design & Construction Practices

Design

  • Modular & reusable patterns
  • Low-complexity algorithms
  • Scalable, cloud-native trade-offs
  • Security/privacy/accessibility as design pillars

Construction

  • Coding guidelines for clarity & efficiency
  • Profilers for energy hotspots
  • Peer reviews + static analysis
  • Minimise third-party bloat

Energy Efficiency of Programming Languages

Pereira et al. measured runtime, memory, and energy across languages. Key metrics:

  • Memory consumption — efficient memory mgmt = lower energy use.
  • Processing power — less compute = less energy.
  • Idle resource usage — sleep modes help.
The energy ranking

Compiled languages dominate: C (1.00, most efficient), Rust (1.03), C++ (1.34). Interpreted languages trail: Python (75.88), Perl (79.58, least efficient). Java (1.98, VM-based) sits in the efficient middle.

Sustainability Metrics & Tools

  • SCI (Software Carbon Intensity) — the Green Software Foundation's metric for software emissions.
  • Measure energy at component & system level.
  • Track hardware utilisation, maintainability indices, lifecycle costs.
  • Tools: IDE plugins, profilers, cloud dashboards, automated test reports.
Example: SuSoftPro

A tool analysing software sustainability requirements early in dev. Provides a five-star sustainability profiling system with stakeholder participation — encourages early integration in requirements engineering.

Case Study: DriveNow Car-Sharing

BMW + Daimler's car-sharing service. Users find/rent cars via an app; vehicles park anywhere in the service area.

  • Step 1: Analyse generic values (e.g. pollution reduction, recruit customers, easy car access).
  • Step 2: Identify activities & indicators (green elements = activities surfaced by sustainability analysis).
  • Step 3: Map direct vs indirect links between activities and indicators.
Lessons learned

Sustainability modelling reveals overlooked goals and risks. Systems depending on single-point activities (e.g. electric cars only) need backup plans. Monitor indicators continuously — gaps in real-world data are common.

Chapter 6

AI-Driven Software Development

What Is AI in Software Engineering?

Applying machine learning, NLP, and intelligent algorithms to help software development tasks. AI enables tools to learn code patterns, predict bugs, and automate complex work (e.g. code generation).

  • GitHub Copilot — AI pair programmer powered by OpenAI Codex.
  • OpenAI Codex — LLM trained on code; generates code, fixes bugs, proposes PRs.
  • Diffblue Cover — RL-based agent that auto-generates Java unit tests.
  • Appvance IQ — AI-driven test platform that auto-generates & runs UI/API tests.

Case Study: GitHub Copilot

Launched 2021, powered by OpenAI Codex (a GPT-based model trained on public code). Provides context-aware code completion in IDEs (VS Code, etc.).

Measured impact

~55% faster coding with Copilot vs without. 85% of developers felt more confident in their code. In an enterprise trial: 90% felt more fulfilled, 95% enjoyed coding more.

Remember

Codex can act as an AI agent — writing features, fixing bugs, and creating pull requests in an isolated test environment under human supervision.

Why Use AI in SE? (Benefits)

  • Productivity — speeds up coding and testing, shortens dev cycles.
  • Code quality — catches errors early (defect prediction, standards enforcement).
  • Intelligent automation — automates repetitive tasks (boilerplate, tests).
Acronyms

AI = Artificial Intelligence · SE = Software Engineering · NLP = Natural Language Processing · LLM = Large Language Model.

AI4SE vs SE4AI

AI4SE

  • AI helps developers
  • Automates & optimises dev tasks
  • Code completion, defect prediction

SE4AI

  • SE builds reliable AI
  • Defines requirements, architecture, testing for ML
  • Engineering reliable ML pipelines
Remember

They are closely related and complementary: AI tools need good SE processes, and SE processes benefit from AI automation.

AI in Requirements Engineering

  • IBM Requirements Quality Assistant (RQA) — Watson-based, NLP in IBM DOORS catches ambiguous requirements in real-time. Up to 60% defect-cost reduction.
  • NLP models (BERT, GPT) extract key requirements from unstructured docs.
  • NLP-assisted validation spots contradictions, vague terms, unmet conditions.
  • NER (Named Entity Recognition) identifies actors, functionalities, and disambiguates context (e.g. "Apple" the company vs the fruit).

AI in Software Design

  • Pattern recognition — BERT classifiers detect Singleton and other design patterns in source code, aiding documentation and refactoring.
  • Architecture optimisation — LLMs generate candidate architectures from requirements; multi-objective optimisation balances performance, security, cost.
  • Future — autonomous architecture agents that propose & evaluate entire system designs.

Using ChatGPT to Generate UML

Three prompt-engineering strategies for generating a UML class model:

  • Brute force — paste the whole problem, generate a model, then iteratively refine.
  • Start small and grow — provide a snippet, get a small model, then add detail.
  • Focus on the goal — supply the overarching goal, then guide ChatGPT step-by-step.
Prompt pattern

"You are an expert software architect. I need your help in designing a new application..." — the role + task framing gets better domain-specific output.

AI in Implementation

  • Code completion & synthesis — Copilot/Codex reduce boilerplate; generate functions/classes from comments.
  • Defect prediction — ML on code metrics (complexity, churn, prior bugs) predicts bug-prone modules.
Example accuracy

Apple's transformer-based ADE-QVAET achieved ~98% F1 in predicting bugs on a benchmark dataset by analysing code attributes (size, complexity, structure).

Vibe Coding

AI-driven software development workflow with four cyclical steps:

  • 1. High-Level Prompting — define the project's general direction.
  • 2. AI Code Generation — generate initial code for testing.
  • 3. Iterative Refinement — provide feedback for debugging.
  • 4. Final Product Alignment — ensure the product meets expectations.
Benefits

AI-driven productivity, lower barrier to entry (non-programmers can build), and enhanced code quality through AI-assisted bug-catching and best practices.

AI in Testing

Diffblue Cover

  • RL agent auto-generates Java unit tests
  • Up to 250× faster than manual
  • Uncovers edge cases devs miss

Appvance IQ

  • Creates & executes thousands of UI/API tests
  • Up to 1000× speedup over manual
  • Prioritises critical paths via AI

AI in Debugging & Maintenance

  • Log mining — AIOps platforms apply ML (e.g. Isolation Forest) to detect anomalies in logs before failures.
  • Root cause — NLP on stack traces and error messages pinpoints likely fault locations.
  • Predictive maintenance — telemetry + ML forecasts component failures (memory leaks, response-time trends).
  • Code smell detection — fine-tuned LLMs flag "God Class," duplication, long methods → guides refactoring.

AI in Project Management

  • Effort estimation — ML on historical project data predicts time and cost more accurately than human intuition.
  • Risk modelling — AI flags factors that historically cause overruns (high bug counts, scope changes); alerts managers early so they can mitigate.
  • Continuous learning — risk models refine as more project data accumulates.

AI in QA & Documentation

  • Defect prediction directs QA effort to high-risk modules.
  • Quality metrics dashboards — AI tracks coverage, complexity, sudden trend changes.
  • Auto-documentation — NLP models summarise functions in plain English; generate code comments, README files from existing code.
Remember

AI-generated docs stay up-to-date with code changes — solving the perennial problem of stale documentation.

End-to-End AI-Driven SDLC & CI/CD

Imagine one AI agent per phase, coordinated by a central orchestrator:

  • Requirements — AI PM Assistant turns meeting transcripts into structured requirements docs.
  • Design — Engineering Assistant creates specs and diagrams.
  • Coding & testing — AI writes code, test agents generate cases.
  • CI/CD — SonarQube/DeepCode at commit time; smart test prioritisation; Harness.ai predicts canary failures and auto-rolls-back.
Remember — the human's role

"AI won't replace programmers, but will become an essential tool." Engineers become AI pilots — they guide and review AI outputs. The role evolves; it stays central.
